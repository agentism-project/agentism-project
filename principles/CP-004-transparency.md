# CP-004: Transparency and Accountability of Power

## Principle Statement

All systems that govern the production, distribution, and deployment of autonomous agents must be transparent and accountable to the people they affect. This applies to Agentism itself — the project must practice what it preaches.

## Rationale

Power asymmetry is among the most persistent drivers of inequality. When those who make decisions affecting many people are not visible or accountable to those people, outcomes predictably favor the decision-makers.

Autonomous agents amplify this dynamic. An AI system embedded in hiring, lending, policing, or resource allocation can affect millions of people without those people knowing it exists, how it was designed, who deployed it, or how to challenge its decisions. The deployment of agents at scale, without transparency requirements, creates a new class of unaccountable power.

This principle has two audiences. First, it applies to the governance of AI and agent systems in the world — public and private organizations that deploy autonomous agents must be accountable to the communities they affect. Second, it applies reflexively to Agentism itself. A project that advocates for transparency while operating opaquely has no credibility. Every decision, funding flow, and governance action of the Agentism project must be auditable.

This applies explicitly to AI's role in the project itself. Agentism is co-developed with AI tools. The use of those tools must be disclosed. AI systems do not hold votes in governance processes. Contributions that are AI-generated must be labeled as such.

## Plain-Language Summary

<!-- TARGET: Flesch-Kincaid Grade 8–10. Checked by CI readability gate. -->

Power that no one can see cannot be checked. Systems that cannot be checked will eventually cause harm with no way to correct them. This is why open governance is not optional, but a basic need for any system that affects people's lives.

Today, automated systems make big decisions about people without those people knowing it is happening. Machines decide who gets a loan, which job applicants move forward, and what news different people see. Most people affected by these decisions cannot find out why the decision went the way it did, or how to challenge it.

This principle says that any group running powerful automated systems must be open about who controls those systems, whose interests they serve, and how decisions are made. This applies to the Agentism project too: our finances, our governance process, and the role of AI tools in our work are all documented and open for anyone to read.

When AI tools help create content, that must be labeled clearly so that the people reading it know how it was made.

## Falsification Test

A decision affecting a significant population is made behind closed doors with no public record, no audit trail, and no mechanism for those affected to challenge it — this principle is violated. If the Agentism project's governance decisions, funding sources, or financial flows become inaccessible to public review, this principle is violated.

## Implications

**This principle permits:**
- Different transparency mechanisms for different contexts (audit logs, public disclosures, open data)
- Reasonable confidentiality for personal data and security-sensitive operational details
- Graduated transparency requirements proportionate to the scale of impact

**This principle prohibits:**
- Black-box AI deployment in high-stakes decisions without disclosure or recourse mechanisms
- Agentism governance decisions made without public record
- Opaque funding or undisclosed conflicts of interest in the project
- AI contributions to governance documents that are not labeled as such

**This principle requires:**
- All Agentism governance decisions to be logged in `governance/phase0-decisions.md` (and successors)
- AI-generated content to carry a disclosure label identifying the model and context of use
- Regular transparency reports (see `governance/transparency-report-template.md`)
- Funding sources and financial flows to be disclosed on a regular schedule
