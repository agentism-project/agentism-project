# CP-002: Deliberate Transition by Collective Design

## Principle Statement

The shift to an agent-based society must be a conscious, inclusive, and deliberate process — not an accident of market dynamics. Society actively chooses and shapes the transition through structured global debate, with all stakeholders participating.

## Rationale

Historically, major economic transitions — the Industrial Revolution, the digital revolution — arrived largely unplanned. The people most affected were rarely the ones making decisions about how to manage the transition. The result was displacement, suffering, and social upheaval that could have been reduced with deliberate collective design.

The transition to agent-based production is different in two critical ways: it is faster than previous transitions, and the stakes are higher. An unmanaged transition could permanently restructure the relationship between humans and economic participation before any safeguards are in place.

This principle rejects the assumption that the "natural" market path is acceptable. Markets are efficient at allocating resources between actors who can participate in them. They are not efficient at distributing benefits to those excluded from participation, nor at managing transitions whose costs are concentrated on populations with limited political power.

The commitment to deliberation does not imply central planning. The process is distributed, iterative, and bottom-up — but it is explicitly NOT accidental.

## Plain-Language Summary

<!-- TARGET: Flesch-Kincaid Grade 8–10. Checked by CI readability gate. Revised 2026-03-05. -->

Big changes are already happening. Machines are taking over many jobs, and this shift will keep going for years to come. The question is not whether it happens, but whether it happens by accident or by plan.

In the past, big economic shifts mostly just happened. The people most affected often had little say in how the change went, and many were left behind with no clear path forward. This principle says the shift to a machine-based economy must be different. It must be designed on purpose, through open debate, with everyone who will be affected having a real say in how it goes.

That includes workers, families, and communities around the world, not just business owners and governments. Designing the change together does not mean that one group controls the outcome, or that all decisions require full global agreement before anything can happen. It means the process stays open and honest, proposals are debated in public, and no group can lock in a major change without going through a fair and open process first.

## Falsification Test

If a major decision about the deployment, governance, or redistribution of agent-generated production is made by a small group (fewer than a representative cross-section of affected stakeholders) without structured public input and at least a publicly accessible period for challenge and debate, this principle is violated.

## Implications

**This principle permits:**
- Phased deliberation — not all decisions need global consensus simultaneously
- Differentiated processes for different scales (local, regional, global)
- Working groups and expert bodies, provided their decisions are subject to public review

**This principle prohibits:**
- Governance structures that concentrate decision-making power without accountability mechanisms
- "Emergency" deployment of major agent systems without any stakeholder consultation process
- Treating the transition as a technical matter outside the scope of democratic input

**This principle requires:**
- All major governance proposals to include a documented public input period
- The project itself to practice structured, open deliberation on all Durable and Immutable-tier decisions
- Mechanisms for participation that do not require technical expertise (see FR-005 in the SRS)
